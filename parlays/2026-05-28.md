### Retrospective — two calibration misses

1. **The anchor was the bust, and via the risk I named.** I rated Tigers ML
   ~61% and made it THE anchor because numberFire had DET at 62.3% — but that
   number was high purely because the OPPONENT's starter (G-Rod, 10.61) was
   awful. DET's OWN starter (Flaherty, 5.94 ERA) made it a shootout, and DET got
   blown out 7-1. I explicitly wrote "Flaherty leaky → live but uncomfortable,
   not a lock" and *still anchored on it*. **Lesson (promoted to CLAUDE.md ML
   checklist):** when the favorite's own SP has a ~5.00+ ERA, discount the ML
   ~5pp and never use it as a parlay anchor; when BOTH SPs are bad it's a
   coin-flip-plus, not a 60% leg. A flagged blow-out risk should MOVE the number,
   not just sit in a footnote.

2. **I demoted the leg that would have won, on a form narrative.** I made White
   Sox ML the ALT (not the primary) chiefly because of the Twins "quietly hot"
   caveat. But a confirmed ace-at-home (Martin, 1.14 home ERA, .569 opp OPS) vs
   an opener game was the stronger, more reliable signal — and it won 6-1.
   **Lesson (noted on the CLAUDE.md watch list):** a "quietly hot" recent-form
   tag does NOT override a clear pitching-matchup edge; recent bullpen form is a
   soft signal next to a confirmed ace. Keep "quietly hot" as underdog value in
   NEUTRAL/soft matchups, not as a reason to fade an ace.

3. **Honest caveat on overfitting:** one 7-1 result doesn't *prove* 61% was
   wrong (variance is real), and the White Sox/Twins counterfactual is one game.
   But the *process* leaned on the wrong leg for a nameable reason (own-SP ERA),
   so the lessons above are about the reasoning, not just the box score.

Net: 5/28 is a **loss** for the day (had been 3-for-3 on 5/25–5/27).
